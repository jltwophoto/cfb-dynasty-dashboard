Michael
The Dynasty King

Heismans
Michael holds the most wins in league history, having built his first run at Navy — a small program he turned into a powerhouse, capturing three national championships with the Midshipmen. However, his legacy carries a significant asterisk: Michael's 2026 national championship came with a direct violation of league sanctions, making him the only coach in dynasty history to break sanction rules to win a title — a decision that stained his program's prestige and remains a sore subject among rival coaches. After being forced out by the sanctions system, he moved to Michigan and then Oregon, where he's now captured an astonishing three national championships with the Ducks — including back-to-back titles in 2034 and 2035. He holds a 6-2 edge over Angry in the league's marquee rivalry, holds a commanding 6-0 record against Gooch and 4-1 against Eagle, and has produced 7 Heisman winners. Most recently, Michael was hit with a recruiting violation for improper contact with athletes, resulting in a 3-year in-season recruiting ban. Despite the sanctions, the wins keep piling up.

The league's most well-traveled coach, having led five different programs across the country. Eagle started humbly at San Jose State before building Cal into a national contender over 5 seasons — highlighted by a dominant 42-17 national championship win over Angry in 2029. A move to Alabama paid off with another title in 2031, beating Michael's Michigan squad 38-28 — but his time in Tuscaloosa ended abruptly when a scandal involving infractions forced him out, violating his contract and cutting short what could have been a longer reign. Eagle's been on both sides of heartbreak in title games, dropping painful losses to Michael's Navy teams in 2026 and 2027. After a stop at BYU, he's now coaching at Notre Dame, looking to rebuild his reputation and add to his legacy.

BigLaw has carved out a reputation as the league's ultimate program builder, specializing in turning overlooked schools into contenders. Starting at Army, then Memphis — where he won a conference championship over Gooch in 2026 — he's proven he can win anywhere. His longest tenure has been at Baylor (5 seasons), where he earned back-to-back Coach of the Year honors in 2028 and 2029 and produced 2 Heisman winners. His only natty appearance was a tough loss to Angry's USC in 2030, but BigLaw plays the long game. After stints at Missouri and Texas A&M, he's now taken over at Tennessee — bringing his program-building mentality to the SEC.

What Gooch lacks in trophies, he makes up for in unwavering loyalty. An incredible 12 seasons at Oregon State makes him not only the longest-tenured coach at a single program in league history, but the only coach to have never left his program — a distinction no one else can claim. While the Beavers haven't broken through to the national stage yet, Gooch has built a reputation as one of the league's premier running back developers, consistently recruiting and producing some of the top RBs in the nation year after year. His 0-8 record against other users doesn't tell the full story — Gooch keeps grinding, keeps building, and keeps his program competitive. Every dynasty league needs its ride-or-die loyalist, and Gooch is exactly that.

The league's most battle-tested coach and Michael's eternal rival. After early stints at Air Force and Vanderbilt, Angry found his home at USC — where he's spent an incredible 6 seasons and built a powerhouse that's produced 3 national championships. His 2028 natty win over Eagle was a classic, and he toppled BigLaw in the 2030 title game. Angry lives in the biggest games — with more head-to-head matchups than anyone in the league — but his 1-4 record in conference championships against Michael remains the thorn in his side. After moving to Ohio State for a fresh challenge, Angry shockingly retired just 3 games into the 2035 season, citing a vague "personal situation." No official explanation was ever given, but sources inside the locker room suspect the NCAA was deep into an investigation of multiple violations that were likely going to result in a lifetime ban from all organized sports. The truth may never come out — but the timing speaks for itself.
